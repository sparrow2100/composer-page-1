Women in Western Music: Feminizing the Classical Canon. Composer 1

Sophie-Carmen Eckhardt-Gramatté

1899-1974 | composer | virtuoso violinist & pianist

Biography: Sophie-Carmen Eckhardt-Gramatté was born in Moscow in 1899. She started learning music and composing at a young age, starting the piano in 1904 and writing her first piano compositions in 1905. Eckhardt-Gramatté studied violin, piano and composition at the Conservatoire de Paris from 1908-1913, and during this time published a composition, an Etude de Concert, in 1910. She went on to study violin in Berlin in 1914, and had performed her own works in several concert tours of Europe by 1919 when she was just 20 years old. She lived in Spain from 1924-1926, where she wrote her first piano concerto, and toured Germany in 1925 and the USA in 1929. In 1930 she moved back to Berlin, and gave up performing to dedicate her life to composition and pedagogy. It was there that she took composition lessons at the Berlin Academy of Arts. She moved to Vienna in 1936 and continued composition there, then moved to Winnipeg, Canada in 1953. There she continued to compose and teach, having as one of her students the renowned Canadian violinist Gwen Thompson. In 1970 she received an honorary doctorate from Brandon University in Manitoba, and in 1974 became the subject of a CBC documentary. Her music and old performances can now be found in Radio Canada International’s Anthology of Canadian Music (ACM).

Compositions: Throughout her composing career Eckhardt-Gramatté utilized many different techniques and styles, including but not limited to serialism, metric manipulation, jazz idioms, neo-classicism, and bitonality. Her ten caprices for solo violin not only demonstrate many of these techniques, but paint vivid pictures of the story behind each one. Additionally, her works are extremely difficult to perform and demonstrate her virtuosity as a performer. Although her music can be considered experimental and forward-thinking, she cites major influences as J.S. Bach and the First Viennese school.

Here is a selection of S.C. Eckhardt-Gramatté's works, which serve to demonstrate her skill in composition on both the violin and piano, and the range of styles her works encompass. The first piece, Le Départ D'un Train, comes with a story which it illustrates: "[it] was written in 1928 in Barbizon, France, shortly after the moving farewell from her sick husband at the railway station in Berlin. Combining the noises of the departing train, running sometimes faster, sometimes slower, driving uphill then down with the reflecting feelings, imagination and will to create, the caprice was born." (-Francis Chaplin) This is one of many caprices for solo violin which are often categorized in the same class as Paganini caprices, which are incredibly difficult and renowned for their virtuosity.
